Navo Solar · Investor Pitch · April 2026

Trust-Embedded Solar Distribution
Through Village Savings Networks

Navo Solar sells solar products to off-grid households in Uganda's West Nile region. Sales happen through commercial agents embedded in existing VSLAs, introduced by community-trusted trainers and financed, where needed, by the group's own loan fund.

01 — The Problem

Energy poverty that isn't being solved
by the models that exist.

The West Nile region — Arua, Yumbe, Adjumani, Obongi, Zombo, Pakwach, and Nebbi — is home to roughly 536,000 off-grid households. While a transmission line reached Arua town in 2024, last-mile rural connection remains years away for the vast majority.

Households spend $6–$15 per month on kerosene — 15–25% of typical monthly income for subsistence farmers earning $1–$3 per day. A $15 pico solar kit pays for itself in one to two months and eliminates ongoing kerosene spend entirely. The value proposition is not aspirational — it's immediately economic.

No grid connection in sight

While a transmission line reached Arua town in 2024, last-mile household connection remains years away. Kerosene traps families in a recurring cost cycle with no exit.

PAYG models don't reach here

Pay-As-You-Go solar requires mobile money, stable income history, and national ID. Refugee families and the most cash-constrained households are excluded from every existing model.

The GIZ consignment failure

In 2011, GIZ tested freelance solar agents in Arua. 46 lamps went unrecovered — not from dishonesty, but from structurally flawed consignment to capital-constrained agents with no accountability mechanism.

Intent exists — fulfillment doesn't

A 2025 RCT across 312 VSLAs found that structured savings sessions increased solar savings goals by 32 percentage points, but produced no measurable increase in solar ownership. The missing link is fulfillment infrastructure.

02 — The Model

Three roles, deliberately separated.

The channel architecture isolates trust, transactions, and financing into distinct roles — directly addressing the role-conflict failure mode CARE flags and the consignment failure mode the GIZ report documents.

01
Trust Layer

Community-Based Trainer

Trust & Information

Introduces the product at a VSLA meeting based on personal use (each CBT receives a demonstration unit 4–6 weeks before launch). Endorses, facilitates group discussion on VSLA loan eligibility, and introduces the agent.

  • ✓ Per-meeting stipend ($20/session)
  • ✓ Receives complimentary solar lamp
  • ✗ Holds no inventory
  • ✗ Handles no payment
  • ✗ Earns no per-unit commission
02
Commerce Layer

Commercial Agent

Inventory & Transactions

Recruited from within the VSLA — a member in good standing, vetted by CBT and group. Purchases inventory upfront (the structural fix to GIZ). Stock is held at a fixed location; agent draws units as customers commit.

  • ✓ Commission per unit sold ($2–3)
  • ✓ Quality bonus at 30–60 days ($1)
  • ✓ Small monthly retainer ($100–150)
  • ✓ Faces VSLA peers weekly — social accountability
03
Finance Layer

VSLA Loan Fund

Customer Financing

Members who cannot pay cash apply for a productive-use loan from their group. Repaid through the existing weekly meeting collection. No mobile money, no external credit assessment, no national ID requirement.

  • ✓ ~99% repayment rate, historical
  • ✓ Pre-existing credit infrastructure
  • ✓ Trusted by members
  • ✓ Venture holds zero credit risk

Why the split is structural, not contractual.

The CBT's stipend is paid per session attended, not per unit sold. If a CBT begins selling directly, their stipend disappears and they must operate as an agent — purchasing inventory upfront, with all the associated capital risk. The incentive structure self-enforces the role boundary without any legal mechanism required.

The pilot is designed to test the core commercial hypothesis — not just to sell solar kits, but to generate publishable data regardless of the outcome. Even a failed pilot produces a documented failure mode.

The Subsidy Lever (Epoch 2+)

UECCC/EASP, launched November 2024, discounts solar lanterns by 60% for registered ESCOs. If accredited, the effective customer price for a pico drops from $15 to ~$6 — dramatically expanding addressable demand. West Nile is explicitly classified as a remote area, adding further per-unit incentives. The programme runs through June 2027, squarely within Epoch 2's timeline.

08 — Evidence Base

Every structural decision traces to
a documented finding or failure mode.

We are not building on assumptions. Each design choice — the upfront purchase, the CBT stipend, the role separation — maps directly to a published study or a documented failure.

"A behaviorally-informed information and savings session embedded in VSLA meetings increased having a savings goal for a solar product by 32 percentage points and increased members contacting certified solar suppliers. However, the study found no measurable increase in solar ownership."
Elven et al., npj Climate Action (2025) — RCT with 312 VSLAs, Uganda refugee settlements
"After eleven months, 46 lamps remained outstanding with technicians who had taken them on credit but never remitted payment. The failure should not be attributed to dishonesty, but to the structural problem of giving consignment inventory to capital-constrained agents who rationally treat it as a liquidity buffer during family emergencies."
GIZ Solar Lamps Field Test, Arua District (2011)
"Entrepreneurs who begin their businesses with a loan have lower sales and drop out sooner than those who pay upfront."
Solar Sister / UNFCCC documentation — Uganda, Rwanda, South Sudan
"Hybrid compensation models with 30–50% performance-based incentives produced the best balance of retention and service quality. Commission-only models (70%+ PBI) produced higher short-term performance but increased stress and reduced retention."
Living Goods Uganda, Frontiers in Health Services (2025) — 1,104 community health workers
09 — FAQ

Frequently asked questions.

Three factors. (1) No realistic near-term grid electrification timeline despite the 2024 transmission line to Arua town. (2) Documented high VSLA penetration through CARE, NURI, and multiple NGO partners operating there for decades. (3) The closest prior attempt (GIZ/Matrix Electronics, 2009–2011) occurred and failed in this exact geography, which means the failure mode is documented and lessons are specific. That is a stronger evidence position than launching in a new geography with no prior data.
The split is maintained by compensation design, not contractual prohibition. The CBT's stipend is paid per session attended, not per unit sold. If a CBT begins selling units directly, their stipend disappears and they would need to operate as an agent — purchasing inventory upfront. The structural incentive is to maintain the CBT role: guaranteed stipend income for attending meetings you are already attending, versus the risk and capital requirement of the agent role. A CBT who wants to transition is free to do so, but cannot do both simultaneously in the same VSLA.
VSLA loan default affects the internal loan fund, not the venture's balance sheet. The venture sells to the customer at the point of transaction — cash or VSLA-financed — and does not hold receivables. VSLA internal loan management (including arrears recovery) is handled by the group according to its own Constitution. The credit risk is internalised by a group with a 99% historical repayment rate.
All products are VeraSol-certified. Sun King and d.light both offer warranties on certified product lines. The agent — present at VSLA meetings every week — is the first point of contact for product failure and is responsible for initiating warranty claims. The quality bonus structure gives the agent a direct financial incentive to ensure satisfaction at 30–60 days. Replacement batteries and parts sold through the same agent network provide both a repair pathway and a secondary revenue stream.
The programme runs through June 2027. Epoch 2 is targeted for Months 6–24 — squarely within the subsidy window. The commercial model is designed to be viable without subsidy (Epoch 1 operates without it). Subsidy accreditation is a margin lever, not a survival dependency. The programme's $638M World Bank funding and government co-implementation make abrupt termination unlikely within the stated window.
Sun King and d.light have existing distribution networks in Uganda including authorised dealers in Arua. But their networks are retail-based — they depend on customers coming to dealers, not on embedded community distribution. They do not operate through VSLA meeting structures, do not have CBT relationships, and do not provide VSLA-internal financing pathways. The competitive moat is not the product (sourced from the same manufacturers) but the channel. A partnership rather than competition relationship with Sun King or d.light as product suppliers is the intended structure.
CARE Uganda, NURI, Action Against Hunger, AVSI, PALM Corps, AFARD, and CEFORD all operate CBT networks in Arua and Yumbe. Outreach to multiple organisations is underway. The Elven et al. (2025) intervention was delivered by VSLA meeting facilitators, not strictly CARE-trained CBTs — suggesting the model can operate with any trusted VSLA facilitator. If no CBT recruitment is achievable, the fallback uses existing VSLA group leaders as information session facilitators, with the commercial agent role unchanged.
The information session design draws directly from the Elven et al. (2025) methodology. The pilot generates conversion-rate data regardless of outcome, and a sub-30% result is itself a publishable finding for CBT-mediated solar sales in West Nile VSLAs.
We have an established distributor relationship and hold buffer stock. The order-on-commitment pilot design option reduces pre-purchased inventory risk. UGX/USD pricing is confirmed with the distributor at order date, and a 5–10% currency buffer is built into wholesale cost assumptions.
Epoch 1 does not depend on accreditation. It is targeted for Epoch 2 entry; 87 companies are already registered, suggesting an accessible process.
